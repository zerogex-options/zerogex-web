Chapter I

Table of Signals.

Nine instruments in the almanac. Each written up like a chapter — with definition, formula, examples, and the one place you'd get it wrong.

i. The Gamma Flip

The single price at which dealer positioning changes signs. Above it, dealers stabilize; below it, they amplify. Every intraday move should be read in relation to this line first.

ii. The Call & Put Walls

Concentrations of open interest that act as magnets and repellents. Priced continuously against dealer positioning, not raw OI.

iii. Dealer Delta Pressure

Net directional obligation dealers accumulate through the session. Turns a fuzzy "market feels heavy" into a signed number you can act on.

iv. The Positioning Trap

An asymmetry between where retail believes price is going and where dealers are hedged. Where the two disagree, the market reveals its intent.

v. Squeeze Setup

Composite score across gamma, skew, and vanna. Flags the regimes in which a small directional impulse compounds into a session-defining move.

vi. Tape Flow Bias

Directional read of aggressor flow, weighted by strike proximity to key walls. Answers "who's leaning on this move?" in a single line.

vii. Vanna & Charm

The second-order Greeks that turn overnight and mid-morning into predictable regimes. Read them, and you stop being surprised at 10:30.

viii. Range Break Imminence

An early-warning gauge of intraday range failure. Combines expected-move exhaustion with dealer re-hedge thresholds.

ix. End-of-Day Pressure

The final-hour re-hedging bias every closing print obeys. Traded well, it's the one hour of the day with a legible edge.

Chapter II

Positioning, drawn.

Every signal in the almanac is rendered as a plate — the number on the left, the picture on the right, the reasoning underneath.

Plate 1 · Gamma exposure profile, hand-hatched · positive above the flip, negative below.

Where dealers must trade against themselves.

The Gamma Flip is the pivot around which every intraday session organizes itself. Above it, dealer books are long gamma — hedgers absorb volatility, ranges compress, moves die on approach. Below it, the machine runs the other way: hedgers must chase, ranges expand, and small impulses become 1% candles before you finish your coffee.

ZeroGEX draws this line, every fifteen seconds, from the option chain that actually printed — not from a stale snapshot at yesterday's close.

"When the market is above the flip, hedge slower. When it is below, hedge sooner. There is no third instruction." — Vol. II, §4

Pair the flip with the Call and Put Walls, and you have a map: three lines on an intraday chart that tell you, with unfashionable clarity, where the money is trying to go.
